### Q3: How does Generative AI, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), differ from earlier forms of AI like rule-based chatbots or even traditional machine learning models in terms of their capabilities and approach to language?

**Type:** conceptual

**What a good answer covers:**
- Generative AI can *create* original content (text, images, etc.) rather than just classifying or responding with pre-defined scripts.
- LLMs leverage the Transformer architecture and attention mechanisms to understand context over long sequences, unlike earlier statistical or RNN models.
- They are pre-trained on vast amounts of unlabeled data, allowing for broad adaptability and a semblance of creativity in generating human-like responses.

### Q4: Explain the role of a tokenizer in the functioning of a Large Language Model (LLM) and why it's a crucial step before the model can process human language.

**Type:** practical

**What a good answer covers:**
- A tokenizer breaks down input text into smaller units called tokens (words, sub-words, or characters).
- It then maps these tokens to numerical indices, converting text into a numerical format that statistical models can process efficiently.
- This numerical representation is essential because LLMs operate on numbers, not directly on raw text, to perform their predictions.

### Q6: Explain the key differences between 'Foundation Models' and other LLMs, and provide an example to illustrate the relationship.

**Type:** conceptual

**What a good answer covers:**
- Foundation Models are trained using unsupervised or self-supervised learning on unlabeled multi-modal data and are very large models based on deep neural networks trained on billions of parameters.
- They are intended to serve as a 'foundation' for other models, meaning they can be used as a starting point.
- An example is GPT-3.5 serving as a foundation model for ChatGPT, where chat-specific data was used to create a tuned version specialized in conversational scenarios.

### Q7: What are 'hallucinations' in the context of LLMs, and why are they a concern for applications like an educational product?

**Type:** conceptual

**What a good answer covers:**
- Hallucinations refer to instances where an LLM produces content that is nonsensical or factually incorrect.
- For an educational product, hallucinations are a significant concern because students might accept incorrect information as fact, leading to misinformation.
- This can negatively impact the reliability of the AI system and the reputation of the product/startup.

### Q8: Describe at least three layers of mitigation for potential harms when building Generative AI solutions, and give a brief explanation for each.

**Type:** practical

**What a good answer covers:**
- Model: Choosing the right model for the use case and potentially fine-tuning with training data to reduce harmful content risk.
- Safety System: Implementing tools and configurations (like content filtering) on the platform serving the model to detect and mitigate harm, including jailbreak attacks.
- Metaprompt: Using system inputs, grounding techniques, or Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) to direct or limit the model's behavior and information sources.
- User Experience: Designing the UI/UX to limit user inputs, display appropriate content, and be transparent about the AI application's capabilities and limitations.

## Module: prompting

### Q9: What is Prompt Engineering and why is it considered a crucial skill for interacting with Generative AI models?

**Type:** conceptual

**What a good answer covers:**
- Prompt Engineering is the process of designing and optimizing text inputs (prompts) to guide AI models toward producing desired and consistent outputs.
- It is crucial because the quality of the prompt directly influences the quality of the AI's response, making it the primary interface for generative AI applications.
- It involves an iterative trial-and-error approach to refine prompts, ensuring desired outcomes for specific application objectives and models.

---

### Q10: Explain the concept of 'Tokenization' in the context of Large Language Models (LLMs) and its impact on prompt engineering.

**Type:** conceptual

**What a good answer covers:**
- Tokenization is the process by which an LLM converts raw text prompts into smaller units called 'tokens' that the model can understand and process.
- The way a prompt is tokenized directly impacts the quality of the generated response, as LLMs are trained on these tokens.
- Different models or versions of a model can tokenize the same prompt in different ways, highlighting the importance of understanding this process for effective prompt design.
